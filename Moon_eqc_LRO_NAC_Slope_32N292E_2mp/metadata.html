Description:
Abstract:
This is a colorized shaded-relief slope map of the Digital Elevation Model (DEM) extracted from the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter Camera (LROC) Narrow Angle Camera (NAC) geometric stereo images from the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter mission. The original data product is a DEM from stereo images acquired at approximately 0.5 m/p resolution, and the DEM is set at 2m/p, downsampled to minimize noise. The DEM covers the Lichtenberg Crater site. The DEM is controlled to the Lunar Orbiter Laser Altimeter (LOLA) dataset. DEM values are in meters and referred to a radius of 1737400 meters.
Purpose:
The NASA Constellation Program (CxP) has identified 50 regions of interest on the Moon that the LROC NAC will image under various conditions. The DEMs are generated from this imagery. High resolution DEMs will be essential for identifying potential landing sites and landing site hazards, planning the placement of landed assets on the surface, as well as other surface operations and navigation. Site-specific DEMs will also serve as a foundation for ortho-projection and control of local/site mosaics.
Supplemental_Information:
The nodata value for the slope-colorized shaded-relief is 0. This product was created using West/East bounding coordinates of 0 to 360 degrees with longitudes increasing to the East. Due to FGDC standards, the bounding coordinates are reported here in the Spatial Domain section in terms of -180 to 180 degrees longitude. Both longitude methods are recognized by the International Astronomical Union (IAU).

Data_Quality_Information:
Logical_Consistency_Report:
For each DEM pixel, a derived confidence value is also assigned in the file named LRO_NAC_Conf_32N292E_2mp.tif. The confidence values used to assess the DEM pixels are: &gt; &gt; 0 = NoDATA, outside boundary &gt; 1 = Shadowed &gt; 2 = Saturated &gt; 3 = Suspicious (edge, corner, did not correlate, etc) &gt; 4 = Interpolated/Extrapolated from neighbor pixels &gt; 10-14 = Value range of successful correlations &gt; 15 = Manually interpolated &gt; The process of DEM extraction by comparing the paired stereo images is never perfect and the resulting DEMs will contain some level of inaccuracies. The random height errors are most obvious in DEMs extracted from low signal-to-noise imagery and tend to give the surface a faceted or exaggerated rough appearance (this is particularly apparent in the shaded reliefs). Localized areas of high noise may result from shadows or other low contrast features. Errors that have more identifiable patterns, unrelated to the image quality, are artifacts of the particular extraction or editing techniques. For example, sections of the DEM that are edited manually, though improved, may still be distinguishable by their smooth texture from the more accurately portrayed natural terrain in other areas (manual edits can also be clearly identified in the confidence maps). Linear artifacts along which the DEM height changes discontinuously by a small amount may also originate from multiple sources in the data processing. For example, such small offsets are commonly seen at the join between data from the two cameras of the LROC-NAC system. Additional rectangular artifacts can be traced to the SOCET SET Next-Generation Automatic Terrain Extraction (NGATE) matching algorithm, which divides the whole DEM into small blocks. For each block, NGATE computes a set of transformation parameters between ground XYZ and image line, sample, and X parallax. The set of parameters are computed based on the sensor model as well as the specific location. As a result, there may be some minor blocking effect (horizontal line and/or vertical line along the block boundaries). Elevation changes across both inter-camera seams and block boundaries should always be less than the expected vertical precision of 1 m. To maintain the maximum scientific integrity of the data and to preserve detail in other areas, no attempt has been made to smooth over these artifacts. To view examples of common matching issues and artifacts please see: <http://pub.lmmp.nasa.gov/redmine/attachments/404/Artifacts.pdf>

Positional_Accuracy:
Horizontal_Positional_Accuracy:
Horizontal_Positional_Accuracy_Report:
To compute the absolute horizontal accuracy, three of the sixteen LOLA tracks crossing the DEM were selected at random for comparison. The data set that the LOLA tracks were chosen from contains tracks that were not crossover corrected. Multiple tracks were sampled to account for this and ensure that offsets were consistent. The DEM was registered to these tracks to calculate the horizontal offset relative to LOLA and therefore find the absolute horizontal offset. The average horizontal offset, relative to LOLA data, is 45.7 meters. The maximum horizontal offset, relative to LOLA data, was 77.9 meters. Since the accuracy of the LOLA data is 20 meters, the maximum absolute horizontal offset for this site is 97.9 meters.
Quantitative_Horizontal_Positional_Accuracy_Assessment:
Horizontal_Positional_Accuracy_Value: 97.9
Horizontal_Positional_Accuracy_Explanation:
The relative horizontal accuracy (pixel to pixel across the DEM) is the same as the spatial resolution of the DEM which is 2m. The absolute horizontal accuracy is dependent upon the absolute horizontal accuracy of the LOLA data. When this DEM was made, the absolute horizontal accuracy of the LOLA data was 20 meters.
Vertical_Positional_Accuracy:
Vertical_Positional_Accuracy_Report:
The absolute vertical accuracy of the slope map is the same as the DEM. To compute the absolute vertical accuracy three of the sixteen LOLA tracks crossing the DEM were selected at random for comparison. The data set that the LOLA tracks were chosen from contains tracks that were not crossover corrected. Multiple tracks were sampled to account for this and ensure that offsets were consistent. The DEM was registered to these tracks to calculate the elevation offset to LOLA and therefore find the absolute vertical error. The average elevation offset, relative to the LOLA data, is 4.81 meters. The maximum elevation offset, relative to the LOLA offset was 9.4 meters.Since the current elevation accuracy for LOLA data is 1 meter, the absolute elevation offset for this site is 10.4 meters.
Quantitative_Vertical_Positional_Accuracy_Assessment:
Vertical_Positional_Accuracy_Value: 10.4
Vertical_Positional_Accuracy_Explanation:
Since the DEM is tied to LOLA, the vertical accuracy is limited by both the absolute vertical accuracy of the LOLA data and the Expected Vertical Precision (the relative precision) of the DEM. The absolute vertical accuracy of the LOLA data is 1 meter. The relative precision of the DEM is expected to be 1 meter, but can be as large as 2 meters.
