Abstract :Physical properties of the lunar regolith vary by depth. The uppermost regolith is composed of finer-grained, highly porous material characterized by low bulk density and thermal conductivity. Density and conductivity increase with depth, following an exponential vertical profile. The steepness of this profile is described by the "H-parameter", where small values of H indicate more compact regolith, and large values of H are consistent with more fluffy, unconsolidated regolith. While daytime surface temperatures on the Moon are primarily controlled by surface energy balance through albedo and emissivity, nighttime temperatures are controlled by the thermophysical properties of thermal conductivity, density, and heat capacity. Nighttime thermal measurements of the lunar regolith using the Diviner instrument aboard the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter were used to derive values of H and map its variation by location.
